There is something uniquely painful about a critical purchase being blocked not by policy, budget, or supplier risk, but by a person who no longer works at the company.

That is the **Zombie Workflow**.

It looks alive in the process diagram. The route still exists. The approval step still shows on the screen. Reminder emails still fire. Audit trail still grows. But the human being the system is waiting for left months or years ago.

And the business waits with it.

**A Situation Too Many Teams Know:**
I have seen multi-million-dollar bridge buys sit idle because the final approval stage pointed to a director who had resigned long ago. The category team assumed the approver was slow. Finance assumed sourcing was late. IT assumed the workflow was functioning. Nobody immediately realized the process had become structurally dead.

This is one of the most embarrassing classes of enterprise failure because the root cause is so avoidable. Yet it happens everywhere.

**Why It Happens:**
Many procurement platforms still rely on hard-coded names or manually maintained approval matrices. During implementation, this feels manageable. The organization structure is known. The approvers are identified. Testing passes. Go-live happens.

Then the real company begins moving.

People resign.
People change roles.
Business units reorganize.
Delegations expire.
Temporary approvers become permanent.

If the workflow engine is not connected to a reliable source of organizational truth, it slowly drifts away from reality.

**The Human Cost Is Bigger Than The Technical Cause:**
I have watched sourcing teams burn days chasing approvals that could never happen. They escalated politely, then urgently, then politically. They sent reminders, called admins, raised tickets, and finally discovered the approver mailbox was inactive.

By then, the supplier had moved on, inventory had shifted, and the internal credibility damage was already done.

This is why experienced procurement leaders care so much about workflow ownership. They have learned that a process map is only as good as its connection to living people and roles.

**A More Grounded Example:**
Suppose a buyer is trying to secure a last-time-buy for a constrained component. The approval chain includes plant, category, finance, and a final operations director. That final approver left eighteen months ago, but the matrix was never updated after a reorganization. The request sits. The buyer assumes someone senior is reviewing. In reality, the software is waiting for a ghost.

That is not just a workflow bug. It is a business continuity failure.

**What Better Looks Like:**
Mature procurement systems should anchor approvals to roles, authority models, and live HR data rather than to static names. They should automatically detect:

- inactive approvers
- vacant positions
- expired delegations
- role changes
- orphaned approval steps

Then they should reroute intelligently and visibly before the business notices a stall.

This is exactly the kind of thing modern automation should solve well. Not flashy chatbot work. Real operational governance. If the platform can monitor who is active, who owns the role, and what thresholds apply, then the process should survive normal human movement.

**The Final Insight:**
Workflows should be resilient to turnover. People leave. Roles change. Organizations evolve. If the procurement engine cannot absorb that reality automatically, then the process is not robust. It is only temporarily intact.
