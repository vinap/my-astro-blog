Lately I’ve been thinking about how much the internet has changed in just a year. We all love using ChatGPT, Gemini, and Google’s AI Overviews for quick answers. But there’s another side to this shift — the impact on the people who create the knowledge.

For years, the open web survived on a simple exchange:
- Creators spend hours researching and writing
- People visit their sites
- Ads and traffic support that work

Now users get answers directly from AI:
- no visit
- no traffic
- no revenue

## Why this matters

Organic click-through rate (CTR) dropped about 61% when AI Overviews appear. Only around 0.61% CTR remained for queries with AI Overviews vs ~1.76% earlier. Some analyses report a ~34.5% drop in clicks for top-ranked pages when AI Overviews are shown.

Even when there’s no AI Overview, CTRs are falling — indicating a broader shift. This creates a strange paradox: AI depends on high-quality human content, but it is slowly cutting off the oxygen supply for the people who produce that content.

## What could happen next

If we don’t adapt, the result could be:
- fewer independent blogs
- more paywalls
- less fresh, freely available knowledge
- fewer voices contributing to open knowledge

I’m not against AI — far from it. But it’s time to think about a more sustainable system:
- licensing content rights
- revenue-sharing for training data
- better attribution and credit for creators

Because if creators stop creating, the whole ecosystem suffers — including AI.
