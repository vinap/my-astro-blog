There are many ways to damage a supplier relationship, but one of the fastest is making good suppliers repeat expensive work because your sourcing software cannot handle change gracefully.

I call this the **Fresh Set Trap**.

If you have run enough RFQs and RFPs, you know this moment well. The event is already live. Suppliers have invested hours, sometimes days, answering technical questions, uploading compliance documents, pricing line items, clarifying lead times, and routing the bid internally through sales, engineering, and legal.

Then someone inside your organization says, "We forgot to ask about MOQ" or "Can we add incoterms by plant?" or "We need a packaging sustainability field before leadership review."

In a mature process, that should be manageable.
In many legacy systems, it becomes a reset button with a smile.

**A War Story From Sourcing Operations:**
I once saw a strategic sourcing event for mechanical assemblies get reset twice because stakeholders kept refining the questionnaire after suppliers had already started. The software could not add new questions cleanly without reopening the structure. So the team did what the platform allowed: reset to draft and reissue.

To the internal team, it looked like a minor administrative inconvenience.
To the suppliers, it looked disrespectful.

One senior supplier contact said something that was painfully accurate: "You are asking us to pay for your process immaturity."

He was right.

**Why This Hurts More Than People Expect:**
Suppliers do not answer complex sourcing events casually. Good responses require effort from multiple functions:

- sales for commercial positioning
- engineering for technical fit
- planning for capacity
- logistics for freight and lead times
- legal for terms review

Every unnecessary reset burns real supplier time. And suppliers absolutely remember which customers waste that time.

That memory has consequences. The best suppliers become slower to respond, less willing to invest, and less enthusiastic about prioritizing your business. Poor sourcing process becomes a hidden supplier tax.

**The Software Design Failure:**
Many legacy sourcing tools were built with rigid sequential states: draft, publish, respond, evaluate, award. That looks neat in a requirements document, but sourcing in real life is iterative. Stakeholders learn as the event unfolds. Questions evolve. Data gaps surface. Commercial discussions reveal new comparison needs.

When the platform cannot support additive change, it forces teams into destructive change.

That is the difference between a system built for workflow control and a system built for collaborative sourcing.

**A More Realistic Example:**
Suppose you launch an RFP for precision-machined housings. Suppliers answer tolerances, tooling assumptions, pricing breaks, and lead times. Midway through the event, operations realizes you also need supplier answers on export packing method and regional MOQ because the award will cover two sites, not one.

If the software is flexible, you add the delta and ask only the new questions.
If it is rigid, you wipe the draft state and force all ten suppliers to start again.

That is not just inefficient. It changes supplier behavior. Strong suppliers with better opportunities will walk away. The vendors who stay are often the ones with excess bandwidth, not necessarily the best capability.

**What Better Looks Like:**
Good sourcing software should let teams revise without disrespecting supplier effort. It should support:

- additive questionnaires
- answer preservation
- version history
- change summaries
- targeted follow-up by question or lot

It should tell suppliers, "Two new questions were added. Your previous responses remain intact." That one design choice dramatically changes the tone of the event.

And from a procurement leadership perspective, that matters. Supplier experience is not separate from sourcing performance. If your best suppliers hate your process, your awards will eventually reflect that.

**The Final Insight:**
After years of enterprise sourcing implementations, I have learned that rigidity is not professionalism. In software, and in sourcing, maturity means being able to adapt without destroying trust. If your platform handles evolving requirements by making suppliers redo work, then the tool is not controlling the process. It is taxing the relationship.
