Legacy enterprise vendors are often generous on the way in and ruthless on the way out. They will help you configure, customize, extend, patch, and integrate their platform for years. But the moment you try to leave, you discover how expensive your own history has become.

That is the **Vendor Lock-in Prison**.

After 15 years around procurement and sourcing deployments, I have seen companies tolerate deeply broken user experiences, brittle integrations, and expensive change cycles simply because the cost of escape felt even worse. The software was no longer being chosen. It was being endured.

**What Lock-In Looks Like In Real Life:**
A procurement organization decides it needs a modern platform. The business case is strong. Users hate the old workflows. Reporting is weak. Supplier collaboration is clumsy. Innovation has stalled. Everyone agrees change is overdue.

Then the migration work starts.

Where is the historical transaction data?
How do we export approval history?
Can we extract attachments, comments, supplier responses, and change logs?
Which APIs are documented?
What is encrypted?
What lives only in proprietary service tables?

That is when optimism turns into procurement archaeology.

**A Case I Still Think About:**
One company was quoted a six-figure services bill simply to get historical procurement and sourcing data exported in a usable structure. Not transformed. Not cleansed. Just exported. Their own commercial history had effectively become a paid feature.

The result was predictable. Leadership delayed migration. Users stayed on the old system longer. Manual workarounds grew. The cost of staying hiddenly accumulated while the visible cost of leaving scared everyone.

This is how software prisons are built: not with walls, but with extraction fees and undocumented dependency.

**Why Procurement Platforms Are Especially Vulnerable:**
P2P and sourcing systems hold messy, business-critical context:

- contracts
- events
- bid history
- approvals
- supplier messages
- invoices
- exceptions
- audit trails

Migrating this is hard even under good conditions. When a vendor stores key context in proprietary formats or hides export capability behind consulting engagements, the customer loses leverage fast.

And from a software deployment perspective, this creates another problem: teams stop modernizing because the migration path looks politically and financially dangerous. The architecture stagnates. The business adapts with human effort instead.

**A More Human View:**
I have spoken with procurement leaders who knew their platform was hurting adoption but still could not push for change because they feared losing history, auditability, and supplier context. They were not defending the incumbent system because they loved it. They were defending continuity because the exit risk was too high.

That is a terrible place for any business to be.

**What Better Looks Like:**
Good enterprise software should assume the customer owns its data completely. That means:

- clean exports
- documented schemas
- accessible APIs
- attachment portability
- audit-log portability
- reasonable migration support

If a platform makes departure nearly impossible, that is not customer success. That is dependency monetized.

I also think this is one place where AI can help pragmatically. Intelligent extraction, UI automation, and semantic mapping can lower the migration burden by reconstructing meaning from legacy screens and attachments when the vendor makes clean extraction difficult.

**The Final Insight:**
Software should improve a company's freedom, not reduce it. If the intelligence of your procurement operation can only live inside one vendor's walls, then the business does not truly own its own capability. It is renting memory from a landlord who controls the keys.
