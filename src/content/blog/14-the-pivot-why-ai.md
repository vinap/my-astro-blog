After 15 years working across procurement platforms, sourcing workflows, supplier integrations, master data, and enterprise deployments, I reached a conclusion that was both uncomfortable and clarifying: the old way of solving these problems is running out of road.

That realization is why I am choosing AI engineering.

This is not a rejection of software discipline. It is the opposite. It comes from years of seeing exactly where traditional enterprise design works and where it repeatedly fails under real-world complexity.

I have spent a large part of my career building structured solutions to structured problems:

- approval workflows
- EDI mappings
- matching logic
- routing rules
- vendor master controls
- sourcing events

Those things matter. They still matter. But the modern supply chain now produces a level of ambiguity, speed, and contextual variability that brittle rule systems cannot comfortably absorb.

**What Experience Taught Me:**
Every story in this series points to the same pattern. The failure is rarely that the system had no logic. The failure is that the logic was too narrow, too rigid, too delayed, or too blind to surrounding context.

The dashboard only knew transmission, not commercial acknowledgment.
The ERP knew PO numbers, not lineage.
The workflow knew approvers, not organizational drift.
The sourcing event knew sequence, not collaboration.
The master data knew strings, not identity.
The risk engine knew internal approvals, not external reality.

When you live around these systems long enough, you stop asking for more forms and more rules. You start asking for systems that can reason.

**Why This Is A Software Pivot, Not A Trend Pivot:**
I am not interested in AI because it is fashionable. I am interested because procurement is full of semantic problems hiding inside transactional systems:

- supplier responses that need interpretation
- part identities that need resolution
- contract language that needs context
- disruptions that need prioritization
- exceptions that need explanation

These are not "just automation" problems. They are understanding problems.

Classical enterprise software expects the world to fit the schema. Real procurement work constantly escapes the schema.

**A More Personal Truth:**
For years, I helped teams patch around those limits. Add one more rule. Add one more status. Add one more report. Add one more manual checkpoint. It works for a while, until the process becomes an accumulation of compensating controls around a model that no longer matches reality.

That is the moment I started realizing we were not modernizing. We were extending the life of systems that fundamentally could not think with the business.

AI engineering, especially around retrieval, reasoning, and agentic orchestration, offers a path out of that trap. Not because it removes process, but because it can finally add interpretation where older systems only added structure.

**What I Want To Build:**
I want to build procurement systems that feel less like administrative tunnels and more like operational copilots. Systems that:

- explain the exception, not just flag it
- rebuild transaction lineage automatically
- read supplier context across documents and messages
- monitor live risk instead of static snapshots
- accelerate buyer judgment instead of replacing it

That is the kind of software I believe the next decade needs.

**The Final Insight:**
This pivot is not about leaving enterprise engineering behind. It is about taking everything hard-earned from 15 years in procurement software and applying it to a new toolset that can finally meet the complexity we have been pretending was manageable all along.
